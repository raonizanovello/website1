The Assessment

The program starts before the first rep.

Every client goes through the same assessment process. It determines your starting path, establishes your baselines, and maps the constraints your program needs to work around. Nothing is assumed. Nothing is templated.

The full assessment takes 60–75 minutes. It happens in your first session — not as a separate intake appointment. By the end, you'll know exactly what your first training block looks like and why every choice was made.

Here's every step, in detail.

01 Movement Screen

~25 minutes

Before loading anything, we need to understand how your body moves unloaded. The movement screen isn't a pass/fail test — it's a map. It shows where you have full range, where you're compensating, and where the compensations have been building long enough to create risk.

Most clients arrive with one or two patterns they've been working around for years without knowing it. A hip that shifts during a squat. A shoulder that can't reach full overhead position. A lumbar spine doing the job the thoracic spine should be doing. These aren't weaknesses to ignore — they're the first inputs to your program.

  • Overhead squat Tests ankle mobility, hip depth, thoracic extension, and overhead stability simultaneously. The single most informative movement in the screen.
  • Hip hinge pattern Loaded and unloaded. Shows whether the client can dissociate hip movement from lumbar flexion — critical for any deadlift or row variation.
  • Single-leg stance Balance, lateral hip stability, and ankle proprioception. Reveals asymmetries that bilateral movements hide.
  • Push/pull mechanics Horizontal press and row patterns. Checks scapular control, shoulder positioning, and upper-back engagement.
  • Thoracic rotation Seated and standing. Determines rotational capacity — directly impacts overhead pressing, carrying loads, and everyday movement quality.
  • Core stability Anti-extension, anti-rotation, and anti-lateral flexion tests. Shows whether the trunk can stabilize under the demands the program will place on it.
What I'm looking for

Not perfection. Compensations, asymmetries, and pain-free range limits. The screen tells me which exercises are safe to load immediately, which need regression or modification, and which problems will resolve on their own once the right training stimulus is in place. It also determines which of the three paths — Engine, Anchor, or Architecture — you start on.

02 Baseline Testing

~15 minutes

The movement screen shows how you move. Baseline testing shows what your body can do right now — under load, under fatigue, and against the clock. These numbers become the reference point for every decision in your program and every progress check going forward.

Without baselines, "progress" is just a feeling. With them, it's measurable.

  • Strength baselines Estimated working maxes for primary movement patterns — squat, hinge, press, pull. Not one-rep max testing; submaximal sets that tell me what your training loads should be from day one.
  • Cardiovascular baseline Resting heart rate, heart rate recovery after a standardized effort, and subjective effort rating. Tells me how much aerobic work your system can handle and how quickly you recover between efforts.
  • Work capacity A short, timed effort that measures how much total work you can sustain before output drops. Directly informs session length, rest periods, and training density.
  • Body composition If relevant to your goals. Simple measurements — not DEXA-level precision, but enough to track meaningful change over months.
What these numbers do

They set every training variable: starting loads, rep ranges, rest periods, session length, and weekly volume. They also determine which path you enter first. A client with strong baselines but poor cardiovascular recovery starts on Engine. A client with good endurance but structural instability starts on Anchor. The numbers decide, not the preference.

03 Schedule Mapping

~10 minutes

The best program in the world fails if it doesn't fit the life you actually lead. Schedule mapping is a structured conversation about your week — not the ideal version, the real one.

This is where most coaching relationships break down. The coach writes a five-day program for someone who can realistically train three days. The client misses sessions, feels guilty, and quits. That doesn't happen here because the schedule is the first constraint, not an afterthought.

  • Weekly availability How many days can you consistently train? Not best-case. Consistently. The program is built around that number.
  • Session windows Morning, lunch, evening — and how long. A 40-minute lunch session is programmed differently than a 70-minute Saturday morning.
  • Travel patterns How often, how long, what gym access looks like on the road. Travel weeks get their own program variants — same training intent, adapted equipment.
  • Stress load Work intensity, sleep quality, existing physical activity (sport, walking, manual work). Training is a stress — it needs to fit inside the total stress budget, not exceed it.
  • Equipment access Home gym, commercial gym, hotel fitness center, or some combination. The program uses what you have — it doesn't require what you don't.
04 Path Assignment

~5 minutes

By this point, the data from the movement screen, baseline testing, and schedule mapping tells a clear story. Path assignment isn't a choice — it's a conclusion. You start where your body needs the most foundational work.

Path A

Engine

Aerobic base and cardiovascular conditioning. You start here when recovery capacity is the bottleneck — your body can do the work but can't recover from it fast enough to do it again.

Assessment signals: Slow heart rate recovery, high resting heart rate, subjective fatigue that persists between sessions, low work capacity relative to strength.

Path B

Anchor

Structural strength and joint stability. You start here when the movement screen reveals compensations that would get worse under load, or when structural weakness is the limiting factor.

Assessment signals: Asymmetries in the movement screen, pain during loaded patterns, history of joint injury, inability to maintain position under moderate load.

Path C

Architecture

Hypertrophy and body composition. You start here when the base is already solid — aerobic health is adequate, joints are stable — and body composition or lean mass is the primary goal.

Assessment signals: Clean movement patterns, adequate cardiovascular baseline, stable joints, body composition as primary stated goal.

05 Program Design

Delivered within 48 hours

After the assessment session, I build your first four-week training block from scratch. Every exercise is chosen for a reason — to address what the screen found, to build from your baselines, and to fit inside your schedule.

The program includes your primary sessions, travel variants, warm-up sequences specific to your movement needs, and clear progression targets for the block. You'll know exactly what you're doing, when, and why.

Your first working session happens within the same week. We train through the program together, I cue and refine in real time, and we calibrate any loads or movements that need adjustment. From there, the coaching relationship is ongoing — the program evolves monthly, and I'm a message away between sessions.

What this looks like in practice

A 52-year-old VP of engineering came in after his doctor told him to "start exercising." He hadn't trained in eight years. He expected to be told to start running.

The movement screen showed limited hip mobility, a thoracic spine that barely rotated, and a squat pattern that loaded his lower back on every rep. His cardiovascular baseline was low but not concerning. His biggest bottleneck was structural — years of desk work had created compensations that would get worse with any endurance program.

He started on Anchor. Four weeks of hip-hinge mechanics, rotational stability, and slow progressive loading. No running. No conditioning circuits. Just rebuilding the patterns his body had lost.

By week five, he moved to Engine — and his aerobic work was built on a body that could actually handle it. By month four, he was in Integration. His doctor noticed the difference at his next checkup. That's what the assessment makes possible.
