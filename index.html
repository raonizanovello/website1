My story

I got into coaching because the programs I saw weren't honest.

I competed in powerlifting. I know what it feels like to live inside a training plan for weeks — not just write one. When you're the one timing your food, managing fatigue under a loaded bar, and trying to keep technique clean while recovery gets narrow, your theories about training get honest very quickly.

The body doesn't care how elegant the spreadsheet looked. It answers only to what it had to survive.

I watched people around me fail not because they weren't disciplined, but because their plans didn't account for the life they were actually living. Demanding weeks. Travel. Inconsistent sleep. The coach was nowhere to be found when the week got hard.

So I built something different. A practice capped at 12 clients — not because of ambition, but because that's the number I can genuinely serve. I write every program myself. I stay reachable. And I tell every new client the same thing: if this isn't the right fit, I'll tell you directly. No hard feelings, no sales pressure.

FAQ

Common questions.

Do I need access to a specific gym?

No. I design every program around whatever you have access to — a full commercial gym, a home rack, hotel fitness centers, or just dumbbells. Your equipment situation is mapped during the assessment and the program reflects it exactly. If your access changes, the program changes with it.

What if I travel constantly or my schedule is completely unpredictable?

That's the standard use case here. Every program I write includes travel-week and minimal-equipment variants built in from day one. If you're on the road three out of four weeks, I build the program around that reality — the weeks at home become the exception, not the rule.

Is this remote or in-person?

Both. Online coaching is fully remote — programming delivered through an app, two strategy calls per month, and direct messaging between sessions. In-person coaching is available in the SF Bay Area and for select clients in Sacramento County. Many clients start online and add in-person sessions later.

What does a typical week actually look like?

Most clients train three days per week, 45–60 minutes per session. A standard week is lower body, upper body, and a full-body day — each with a travel alternative. Session frequency, length, and structure are all determined during the assessment based on your actual calendar.

How does the initial assessment work?

It happens in your first paid session, after the consultation. I run a movement screen covering mobility, stability, and motor control, establish strength baselines, and map your schedule. The whole thing takes 60–75 minutes. Your first written program is delivered within 48 hours.

Why is there a 3-month minimum?

Not because of a contract — because assessment-driven training takes time to produce meaningful results. The first month is about building the baseline and learning how your body responds. Most of the real progress — strength gains, movement improvements, habit formation — happens in months 2 and 3. One month isn't enough to do this work honestly.

How many clients do you take on?

Twelve, total — SF and Sacramento combined. This is the number I can genuinely serve: fast response times, specific feedback, and enough attention to actually know what's happening in your training. As of Q2 2026, 2 spots are open. Email me to confirm current availability.

I've never trained seriously before. Is this right for me?

Yes — and this is actually where an assessment-first approach matters most. Most beginners fail because their programs start too hard, too fast, before the body has earned the right to handle that intensity. The assessment catches what needs attention before it becomes an injury, and the programming starts at a volume you can actually absorb, recover from, and repeat. That foundation is what makes everything after it possible.
