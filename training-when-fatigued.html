How to train when you're running on empty

Not injured. Not sick. Just depleted. The right answer is usually not the one you expect.

Ronnie December 2025 4 min read
A program that works on hard days, not just perfect ones. Book a free intro call →

There is a specific kind of day — not injured, not sick, just depleted — where everything about the training session feels optional. Work has been heavy. Sleep has been irregular. You know the session is on the calendar, but every signal says it is the wrong time.

The question most people ask is the wrong one. It is not "should I push through or rest?" It is "what kind of session does the available energy actually support?" That framing has a clearer answer, and it almost always points toward training — just differently.

The worst choice is treating every difficult day as a reason to skip. The second worst is training through it the same way you would on a fresh day.

On a low-energy day, the main lifts are still worth doing — at reduced load and lower volume. Technical quality matters more on these days, not less. A well-executed set at 70% of your working weight gives you more useful signal than a grind at 90% that breaks down after the second rep and leaves you worse than before you started.

What to remove: intensity work, anything requiring significant concentration to execute safely, and conditioning that pushes into high-intensity cardiovascular ranges. What to keep: the primary movement patterns, the structural accessory work your body actually needs, and the habit of showing up consistently.

A program that only functions when life is ideal is a program designed for a life no one has. Knowing what to do on hard days is not a workaround or a lesser version of training. It is part of having a real approach — one that accounts for the full range of weeks, not just the good ones.
